# Panvitium: Lore Bible (author canon)

Single source of truth for what the world *means* and the raw material every piece of written
content draws from (emails, phone messages, flavor text, character voices). This is an
**author-facing** document. A large part of what is true here is never told to the player.

**Relationship to the other docs.** `01` and `02` own what the game *is* and how the loop runs, `03`
owns the content catalog, `04` owns the build decisions. This bible owns the fiction underneath all
of them. Where this bible and `01`'s premise wording disagree, this bible wins (see §1).

---

## 0. The one rule that governs everything: two layers

The world has a **hidden truth** and a **spoken surface**, and they must never touch on screen.

- **The hidden truth** is a cold, almost physical account of what souls and powers actually are
  (§3). It explains *why* everything behaves as it does. It is for the author only. The player never
  reads it, infers it at most, and is never given confirmation.
- **The spoken surface** is wholly theological and occult: souls, Princes, Heaven and Hell, sigils,
  the altar, the descent (§4). This is the only register any character, menu, or message ever uses.

If a line of content would let a careful reader *name the mechanism*, it has failed. The truth is
worth more felt than explained. See §4 for the banned vocabulary and §10 for how to write to this
rule.

---

## 1. Premise

You are a single living human, a person of means, and already damned. You are not climbing toward a
verdict. You are not waiting for death to collect a reward. You corrupt and cull human souls, and
you descend a found altar across many lifetimes (*Katabasis*) to rise through Hell's hierarchy while
still breathing. The end of the road is to become its apex yourself (§7).

There is no arrival and no deferred sentence.
You become the thing while alive, and death changes nothing because the soul is already
what it will be. It is a **living apotheosis**, not a posthumous one.

**Tone.** Grim-baroque, straight-faced, never parody. Latin and pseudo-ecclesiastical vocabulary is
the product's voice. The theology is treated with the seriousness of a medieval grimoire even when
the underlying mechanic is an idle-game number.

---

## 2. The protagonist ("you")

- **A lifelong occultist with money.** You had the means to spend a whole life in study, and you
  did. That is why you stand apart: the knowledge you hold is rare to the point of being
  undocumented, so there is no school and no network around you.
- **A misanthrope who enjoys cruelty.** You hate people and you take pleasure in hurting them. The
  climb is not abstract ambition. It is the pursuit of the worst hurt there is, because to disturb a
  soul *forever*, to deny it any rest, is the cruelest thing one can do to a person, and you want to
  do it to as many as possible, as permanently as possible.
- **Alone, but not without an equal.** You belong to no community of corruptors, but you are not the
  only master alive. At least one other has reached the same path by their own undocumented means.
  That single **adversary** crosses you and competes with you (§9). Beyond them, no peer touches your
  story.
- **Always this, never fallen.** You did not fall into damnation; you were always made for it. The
  occult and the hurting of people were lifelong pleasures, and when you came by the altar it was
  simply a fit, an instrument matched to a nature already whole. No tragedy, no turning point, only a
  man who found the one thing that fit his hand.
- **Above your acolytes.** The faithful are not innocents and are not loyal. They are lesser
  practitioners in your own vein, with you out of fear of you or hunger to shelter under your wing for
  the power it lends. They do the work's lower offices and keep vigil, but in your absence the
  unfaithful even loot (§9). They grasp pieces only, never the whole: not the cosmology, not
  what the altar truly is, not what you are becoming. You gain them, and hold them, as your influence
  grows.

## 3. The hidden cosmology: NEVER SURFACED

> **Author only. Not one word of the vocabulary in this section ever appears in player-facing text.**

A soul is a persisting disturbance in an unknown field, a wave that outlasts the body. When the body
dies, the wave remains. It does not return to a body. Nothing does. Death is one-way.

Two regimes:

- **"Heaven" is rest.** Souls that settle into the field's lowest, steadiest states stop varying.
  This is what the surface calls Heaven, and it is what the **Higher Powers** are: extremely low,
  effectively unchanging states.
- **"Hell" is restlessness.** Souls held in high, variable states that will not settle. This is what
  the surface calls Hell. The **Lower Powers**, the Eight Princes, are specific disturbances that
  perturb other souls (living and damned alike) and keep them from settling.

Load-bearing consequences:

1. **No true floor or ceiling.** The field runs open in both directions. There is always a louder
   disturbance and a stiller rest somewhere past the edge of any one life. "Highest" and "lowest"
   are only the extremes the game renders, never absolutes. Anything can be surpassed; that simply
   happens off the edge of the game.
2. **Allocation is permanent.** A restless soul does not settle on its own, and a settled soul
   cannot be pulled back into disturbance. Once a soul is committed to a pole it stays there. No
   take-backs, in either direction. The contest is therefore only ever over the **uncommitted**: the
   living, and the not-yet-settled dead.
3. **Souls are mass, not score.** Every soul in a state strengthens that state, and whoever holds
   it. More souls thrown into disturbance grow disturbance overall, and grow *you*. This is why
   souls are the resource: your hoard is literally your power.
4. **The ceiling.** Even the largest possible disturbance cannot reach a soul at rest. Rest offers a
   disturbance nothing to grip. So the saved are untouchable forever. You can torment every restless
   soul that lives or is damned and never lay a finger on the ones who got out. For a protagonist
   whose pleasure is cruelty, this is the wound the whole cosmos is built around: the souls you
   would most love to torment are the exact ones you can never reach.
5. **The Church serves rest.** The Church is an instrument of the Higher Powers. The service is
   unwitting at the parish level and more knowing the higher one climbs. Mechanically the Church is
   weather, not a war: it competes for the uncommitted (pulling the living toward rest before you
   can unsettle them) and surfaces against you only in rare bad outcomes. It is never a faction you
   besiege.

## 4. The surface theology: what the player sees

Everything above is spoken only in these terms:

- **Souls** (the resource), bound to **sigils** of the Goetia or given to the **Princes** as
  Devotion.
- **Heaven** and **Hell**, felt as **stillness / rest** versus a **restlessness that will not
  settle**. Stated as condition and mood, never explained as a process.
- The **Princes** as living powers you give your Devotion to, the **sigils** as seals that bite when
  fed.
- The **altar** and the **descent** (§5).

**Banned vocabulary (never appears anywhere a player can read it):** vibration, frequency, wave,
field, energy, mode, ground state, rest state, resonance, amplitude, oscillation, spectrum, or any
near-synonym that frames the soul as physics. The *feel* of those ideas is allowed; the words are
not. "Her soul will not lie still" is good. "Her soul's frequency stays high" is a failure.

**Latin stays untranslated** and is the product's voice (`Opera`, `Suasio`, `Katabasis`,
`Panvitium`, the Prince and sigil names, etc.).

## 5. The altar and the descent

- **Found, not made.** You did not build the altar. You found it, and you alone could read its
  highest use. Another finder would have put it to some small purpose, or failed to understand it,
  or opened it and been **taken**, or **died** on it. Your life of study is what lets you ride it and
  return.
- **A rare, undocumented catalyst.** Channels this strong are vanishingly rare and demand a master's
  understanding to use, which is exactly why none of this is written down anywhere and why the
  Church has no idea the altar exists. Any other master on the same path reaches their own end by
  their own undocumented means, never by sharing yours. There is a powerful channel downward beneath
  the parish and no record of it in the world.
- **The stone.** Its edges are not worn though many have lain on it, and its markings are scoured
  past reading. The wear is on the **way down**, a trail tilted too steeply to climb, smoothed by
  everyone who ever went before. Some of those old stains are not predecessors who succeeded but
  people who found the thing and were not enough for it.
- **The descent.** Lying on the altar drops you into a deep trance that lasts **months**. The body
  stays in near-stasis, spending almost no energy and needing little maintenance, kept by the
  acolytes as a group. The soul goes down to give Devotion and to bind souls to sigils.
- **Liquidate and rebuild.** A months-long absence rots everything you hold and cannot manage, like
  your opaque vice investments, so you sell every vice-related investment / business you cannot manage
  yourself before each descent. Gideon manages the visible and legitimate-looking businesses to
  provide sufficient cash to maintain operations while you go under, and you rebuild almost from nothing
  on the way back up. The real engine of wealth is the vice industry and for that you, your influence,
  your occult work and your hand are needed. Without you, absent for months, they wither no matter who
  minds the desk. Worse, your household staff and even you own acolytes loot in your absence (§9), so a standing empire
  could be taken from your hands or stripped, not merely neglected. Cash is a durable store you more easily secure; at the start
  you accept the looting of what is portable and crystallize the rest before it evaporates.
  What cannot be sold is what scatters, the corrupted you were cultivating and the standing you held,
  all to be won again. The periodic cash-out is a visible pattern for people who can link the vice businesses and investments to you,
  a tell the wrong people can read (see §11).
- **The two lines.** Descent: ***Exspes in ima*** ("hopeless, into the depths"). Rising: ***Auctus
  ex imis*** ("augmented, out of the depths"). They are a matched pair. The rising line carries a
  quiet survivorship: coming back at all is the part a lesser soul could not do.
- **Being taken is not your risk.** It is strictly the fate of the unready. Your mastery has shut
  that door for good: no descent of yours ends in possession or death. It survives only as backstory
  and as the menace under the altar's old stains, never as a fail-state in play.
- **You age, and each descent wastes you.** You are not immortal and you do not stop aging. You age
  normally above ground. During katabasis the near-stasis and minimal energy use pause aging for
  those months, but the body, motionless and unexercised, comes back wasted: muscle gone, weight
  lost, gaunt. So you rise augmented in soul and diminished in flesh, and must rebuild the body as
  you rebuild the estate. The recurring vanish-and-return-wasted is one of the things the wrong
  people notice (see §8, §11).

## 6. The Eight Princes

The powers the whole climb is organized around. You do not bargain with them and you do not fight
them. You **show and give Devotion**, and you rise or stall by their regard. Each is a school of vice
with its own temper, pacing, and economy. In the hidden layer each is one specific disturbance; on
the surface each is a named power that receives Devotion in silence.

**What their regard buys.** Rising is not an abstract score. Each rung is more power in the living
world and a wider capacity to inflict suffering: dominion over more people and more souls. The climb
rewards exactly the appetite you came with, which is why it suits you.

**Hell has no voice.** The Princes never speak to you, never summon you, and never offer contracts.
Their presence reaches you only through what your Devotion buys, never through dialogue. The one
infernal-adjacent presence you do interact with is your own invocations (§9).

| Cardinal Sin (Latin) | English | Prince |
|---|---|---|
| Gula | Gluttony | Beelzebub |
| Luxuria | Lust | Asmodeus |
| Avaritia | Greed | Mammon |
| Tristitia | Sorrow | Leviathan |
| Ira | Wrath | Satan |
| Acedia | Sloth | Belphegor |
| Vanagloria | Vainglory | Rosier |
| Superbia | Pride | Lucifer |

Below the Princes sit the lesser powers of the Goetia (the 72 sigils), entities you bind souls to,
not powers you give Devotion to.

## 7. Semet / the Eternal Sin

The terminal state. *Semet* (Latin, "oneself") is **you** become the largest disturbance there is:
the eight Prince disturbances summed into one. The in-game **Panvitium** ritual (all eight Sins at
once) is the same combination run *temporarily*, a foretaste of what Semet becomes for good.

- **Reached alive.** There is no death-transition. You become Semet while breathing, and once you
  rise above the Princes your living soul is the highest disturbance there is, **for the moment**
  (nothing is a true maximum, §3.1).
- **Not stillness.** An earlier framing called this "going still." Wrong. Semet is maximal *unrest*.
  The "a little less itself each time" note survives, but reinterpreted: the singular self is
  overwritten by the eightfold sum, dissolved into the composite roar, not fading toward calm but
  away from being anyone in particular.
- **Still bounded by the ceiling.** Even Semet cannot perturb a soul at rest (§3.4). The summit of
  the whole climb arrives, and the saved remain beyond it. That is the intended final taste: total
  victory over everything restless, and a wall it can never cross.

The reveal is a milestone and a credits beat, not an exit from the game's post-reveal state.

## 8. Setting, period, and medium

The setting is **circa 2015**. You are a person of means who studies the occult and keeps your Lair,
a **mansion** built around an ancient found altar. The wealth makes a large, isolated, incurious
house unremarkable; the altar sits in a lower room, and the **Studio** holds the PC and the phone.
The era is dressed in degraded retro-digital horror: the interface is cursed and Win95-adjacent in
style (see `02 §10` and `01`'s visual identity), a deliberate aesthetic laid over a mid-2010s
reality, not a literal 1990s period.

**The work.** You corrupt and cull the abstract "reprobates" by three means at once: the vice
industries you own and run, the invocations you set upon chosen people, and your own direct hand. The
reach is **global**, not local; the mansion is only the seat. Every uncommitted soul in the world is,
in principle, contested ground.

**Where it begins.** The game opens at the moment the Lair is newly built and the climb is just
starting. The long consequences develop over play, not at the outset: the months-long absences pile
up, the periodic liquidations form a pattern, you return from each descent visibly wasted, and the
suspicions of Fausto, Reuben, and the Church's investigator (§11) escalate from nothing toward
certainty. Turn one is fresh ground.

**Communication runs through two devices.** A **PC** carries the email system. A **smartphone** sits
on the desk of the **Studio** room and carries its own messages. Both are 2015-era, and both are
incoming-content channels (see §10). Use the difference: email for longer, formal, business and
clerical traffic; the phone for shorter, more immediate, more personal pings.

## 9. Factions and the cast framework

Named characters are **yours to write**. This section gives only the rules any character must obey
and the role-slots already known to be wanted, left unnamed on purpose.

**Hard constraints on any character.**

- **One equal: the adversary.** You have no community and no network, but you are not the only
  master alive. At least one other walks the same path, reached by their own undocumented means, and
  this adversary crosses and competes with you as a real equal, not a rumor. Beyond this single
  rival, no other corruptor at your level appears in your story.
- **The acolytes are lesser practitioners, not innocents.** Occult-inclined people in your own vein,
  directly involved in your evil deeds. They are with you out of fear of you or hunger to shelter
  under your wing for the power it lends, never loyalty. So during katabasis some steal, betting either that
  you will not wake or that you will not know it was them, and preferring a sure theft now to a share later.
  This eases as your influence grows, both in the numbers (the mechanic) and in the fiction: the more terrible
  you become, the fewer dare. They cannot descend, and they grasp pieces only, never the cosmology or what you are becoming.
- **Trusted minions are not acolytes.** The legitimate-looking side, the money, the businesses, the
  cover, is run by mundane functionaries who take no part in the occult work. Trusted, useful,
  kept at arm's length from the altar. Gideon is one (§11).
- **The Church serves the Higher Powers** (unwitting below, knowing above), does **not** know the
  altar exists, and is weather rather than a foe for most of the game, pressing on you only at the
  edges and in rare bad outcomes. The exception is its menacing investigator (§11), who builds a case
  over the long game and turns the local clergy active against you in the endgame.
- **Hell is silent.** The Princes are powers you give Devotion to and rise by, never figures you
  bargain with. Hell does not speak, summon, or offer contracts, and the Princes never address you.
  The lesser Goetia are sigils you bind souls to, not voices.
- **Your invocations can be interacted with.** The entities you summon are yours, and you sometimes
  deal with them directly. They are the one infernal-adjacent presence with any back-and-forth,
  distinct from the voiceless Princes and the silent Goetia.
- **The contested are abstract by default.** The living and the unsettled dead are populations and
  probability inputs, not individuals, unless one is deliberately elevated into a character.

**The realized cast** that fills these roles (priest, bishop, adversary, the one who suspects, the
household, and more) is in §11. Any character added later must obey the constraints above.

## 10. Writing game content from this bible (emails, phone messages, all written text)

Every written artifact obeys §0 and §4: theological and occult on the surface, never the hidden
mechanism. Below is the working guide for deriving content, with the PC email system and the Studio
smartphone as the first cases.

**Voice matrix (who reaches the player, and how).**

- **The household.** Servile, fretful, practical, grasping the truth only in pieces. Reports on the
  vigil, the estate, the missing silver, a sick acolyte, a strange noise from the cellar. Warmth and
  small loyalty, which makes the rest colder. More phone than email.
- **The world / commerce.** Business correspondence, invoices, opportunities, the mundane machinery
  of a wealthy modern operator. The cover under which corruption is done. Some of it is bait you
  recognize as an opening; some is genuinely dull, which is the point. Mostly email.
- **The Church.** Oblique and, from the parish, never about *you* specifically (it does not know
  about you or the altar). A parish notice, a request for a donation, a sermon quoted, an inquiry
  that brushes too close by accident. Pressure that reads as coincidence. The exception is the
  investigator (§11), whose attention is squarely about you and sharpens toward the endgame. Email.
- **The adversary.** The one peer who knows what you are because they are the same. Taunts, feelers,
  false courtesies, the occasional move against you. The only sender who can speak the truth plainly,
  because they share it. Phone or email, sparing and sharp.
- **The one who suspects.** Increasingly unhinged messages that happen to be correct. Counts your
  absences, notes the wasted state you come back in, recites things he should not know. Dismissible
  until he is not. Either channel.
- **Your invocations.** Some reach your inbox: the Doppelgänger and Midas send email (§11). Others
  you deal with only in the Lair and the work. Keep their register apart from the silent Princes:
  yours, summoned, answerable, never warm.

**Rules for any message.**

1. Stay in period (mid-2010s) and in the occult/theological register on the surface. No hidden-layer
   vocabulary, ever (§4).
2. Senders write at their own level of knowledge. The household and the world know nothing; the high
   Church knows more than it says; the suspect knows too much and is trusted by no one; the adversary
   knows exactly what you know. Hell writes nothing, ever.
3. The truth may be *brushed*, never confirmed. A coincidence, a turn of phrase, a thing the suspect
   says that lands wrong. The reader can infer the shape and is never told they are right.
4. Cruelty is the protagonist's pleasure, so content that lets the player do harm should let the
   harm feel *chosen*, not incidental.

**Seed types (PC email and Studio phone).** Business / opportunity (corruption openings dressed as
commerce), household report (estate, vigil, faithful), mundane noise (filler that hides the live
ones), the adversary's contact (taunt, feeler, move), the suspect's escalating warnings, and a brush
from the Church. Tone, length, literacy, and channel should track the sender per the matrix above.

## 11. The cast

The current core roster. Names and region are placeholders; relocalize as needed. All obey the
constraints in §9, and voices and channels follow the matrix in §10.

**The household**

- **Gideon Reyes**, the trusted minion. Not an acolyte and no part of the occult work. He runs the
  legitimate-looking side of your businesses, which is the lesser part so to not raise suspicions.
  Knows you have other investments which he's not managing and that there's a cause for it. He does
  not care as long as his part of the cake remains.
  Loyal, useful, kept clear of the altar and the occult. Voice: logistics and cover, an overdue account,
  auditors sniffing.
- **The acolytes** (unnamed group). Lesser practitioners in your own vein, the hands of your evil
  deeds: the corrupting, the culling, the rites. They are held by fear and by appetite for the power
  of being yours, not loyalty, so in your absence the unfaithful sometimes even loot. Their number, and
  their nerve to stay, grow with your influence. They grasp pieces, never the whole.

**The adversary (your one equal)**

- **Fausto Cescru.** Another master on the same path, reached the opposite way. You found a natural
  catalyst and read it alone; he inherited a lineage, a teacher, a stolen library, and spent a
  fortune trying to build what you got for free. That envy lives under every courtesy. Where you are
  an artisan working one perfect channel in private, he industrializes: institutions, media, a
  network of lesser hands, souls as inventory rather than pleasure. He is joyless and efficient; you
  are a sadist. Because allocation is permanent, every soul he takes is one you never can. He
  deduced you exist because only an equal could read the signs, and the periodic liquidation is one
  of those signs: a man cashing out his whole estate on a cycle is a tell only a fellow descender
  reads correctly. ALtough his katabasis are much more longer than yours as his catalyst (unkown) is
  not as worn as yours. He makes contact, first courtesies, tests the waters so to confirm his suspicions,
  then a proposal to carve up the world's uncommitted (probably a trap), then moves when you decline.
  The only sender who speaks the truth to you plainly, and the only force that genuinely contests you.
  He grows more aggressive and menacing as the climb goes on: the politeness burns off, the feelers
  become threats, and the threats become open moves against you.

**The Church (weather early, an active threat by the endgame)**

- **Father Tom Brennan**, the parish priest. Comfortable, faintly worldly, keeping his faith by not
  looking too hard. You are a patron, which amuses you and keeps the parish incurious. He likes you,
  which is its own small horror. Serves the Higher Powers without the faintest idea he serves
  anything. Harmless until the investigator gets to him; in the endgame his fondness curdles into a
  betrayed man's resolve. Voice: bulletins, thanks for the donation, an invitation you decline, once
  an accidental brush about a cold that will not leave the old chapel.
- **Bishop Adelard Crane.** Urbane, political, the diocese's operator. The knowing end of the axis:
  he half-grasps that the Church serves something colder than mercy and is at peace with it because
  it serves his power too. Would take your money and ask nothing, until the investigator makes
  ignoring you the greater risk; then the same cold calculation turns against you.
- **Father Emil Stahl**, the investigator. The Church's menacing edge, drawn in because the deaths
  around you finally caught the right kind of attention. Where Brennan is comfortable and Crane is
  political, Stahl is the genuine article with teeth: an exorcist and hunter who takes the
  supernatural in deadly earnest and does not like you from the first handshake. He arrives quiet,
  builds his case across the long game, and is the engine that turns Brennan and Crane against you
  for the endgame, the moment the Church stops being weather and becomes a foe. Voice: courteous,
  probing, cold, escalating from polite inquiry to open accusation.

**The one who suspects**

- **Reuben Marsh.** A stonemason on the Lair's construction who touched the altar once and came away
  sensitized and slowly unspooling. He feels the disturbance and cannot name it; on the surface he
  has only the symptoms, a sound that will not stop, a pull, dreams with the same room in them, his
  hands going cold near certain stone. He tracks your long disappearances and the deaths that cluster
  near your work, and he is right about all of it. Believed by no one, sometimes not even himself.
  The closest the world comes to naming you, in a mouth no one will trust. Voice: escalating messages
  and dossiers, half-mad and half-lucid. His emails carry coded easter eggs, the sense-beneath-the-
  raving: acrostics where the first letters of the sentences spell something, and ciphers of that
  kind. If the fourth wall is ever broken, it breaks in one of Reuben's emails, never anywhere else.

**The hope in the dark**

- **Maren Holt**, a palliative nurse who sits with the dying. Not an antagonist and not a force you
  track. She should appear perhaps twice in the whole game, almost in passing. She has no idea you
  exist, yet she quietly brings the dying to peace, and the few she settles are gone to rest, past
  anything you will ever reach. Use her sparingly, as the single faint note of grace in a dark world:
  a parish line, an obituary that reads "passed peacefully," and little more.

**The invocations you deal with**

- **The Doppelgänger.** Two hours after you invoke it, if it is still active, it sends you an email
  from an address almost identical to your own. The uncanny double, writing to you as you. Quiet,
  wrong, and never to be mistaken for a sender from the world.
- **Midas.** Sends you email that tears into your investments and event structure and tells you how to
  do it better. Crude, profane, and personal: he swears, he insults, he points the finger at you.
  Real advice in the mouth of something that despises your incompetence.

**How they connect.** The pre-descent liquidation and the long absences are hinges: Fausto reads it correctly as a fellow
descender, Reuben reads it as proof of the thing he cannot name. The deaths around your work are the
other hinge, and they are what eventually draws Stahl. Two readings of the cash-out, and a body count
that brings the Church.

**Suspicion is an arc, not a starting state.** At the newly built Lair (§8) none of them are onto you
yet. Each accrues evidence as the descents repeat and the absences, liquidations, and wasted returns
pile up, with Stahl's case and the endgame turn of the Church as the late escalation. The entries
above describe their developed state, the place the arc is heading, not turn one.
